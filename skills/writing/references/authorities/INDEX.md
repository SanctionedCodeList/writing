# Writing Authorities

Summaries of foundational texts on legal and professional writing.

## Legal Writing

### Bryan Garner
- **Legal Writing in Plain English** — Plain language principles, nominalization elimination, active voice advocacy
- Key insight: Complex ideas don't require complex sentences

### Eugene Volokh
- **Academic Legal Writing** — Five criteria for scholarly contribution (novel, nonobvious, useful, sound, seen as such)
- Key insight: Test proposals against hypothetical cases

### Fajans & Falk
- **Scholarly Writing for Law Students** — Thesis development, critical reading ("talk back" to texts)
- Key insight: Find the unresolved question, not just the topic

### Christine Coughlin et al.
- **Modern Legal Scholarship** — Analysis over recitation, topic decomposition
- Key insight: Break subjects apart to find original contributions

### Irene Berenguer
- **The Legal Scholar's Guidebook** — Preemption handling, iterative research
- Key insight: Adjust rather than abandon preempted ideas

## General Writing

### Joseph Kimble
- **Writing for Dollars, Writing to Please** — Empirical evidence for plain language
- Key finding: Plain language improves comprehension, compliance, and efficiency

### William Zinsser
- **On Writing Well** — Clarity, simplicity, cutting clutter
- Key insight: Writing is rewriting

### Barbara Minto
- **The Pyramid Principle** — Lead with the answer, MECE organization
- Key insight: Start with the conclusion; support with grouped arguments

## Cognitive Science Sources

### Daniel Oppenheimer
- "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular" (2006) — Complex vocabulary reduces perceived intelligence
- Key finding: Simple words signal competence

### Reber & Schwarz
- Processing fluency research — Easy-to-read text judged more true
- Key finding: Fluency is a heuristic for truth

### Clark & Haviland
- Given-new contract (1977) — Information ordering affects comprehension
- Key finding: Start with known, end with new
